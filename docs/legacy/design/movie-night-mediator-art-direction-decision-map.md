# Movie Night Mediator Art Direction Decision Map

## Purpose

This document captures the higher-level art direction decisions that sit above normal UI polish.
It exists because the founder wants a product feel closer to a top-tier consumer app release, not just a cleaned-up prototype.

## Core principle

We are not trying to copy a reference app or theme pack literally.
We are trying to understand what makes elite apps feel elite, then translate those qualities into a visual world that fits Movie Night Mediator.

## What strong modern apps are usually doing

The strongest modern apps tend to do five things well.

1. They create a coherent visual world instead of a loose collection of screens.
2. They use premium materials with restraint.
3. They make dense information easy to scan instantly.
4. They create one or two signature moments that feel ownable.
5. They apply the same point of view consistently across layout, type, motion, controls, and copy.

## Decision layers

The most important realization is that we are now working across several design layers.
If we skip these layers and only tweak screens, the product will keep feeling like a prototype.

### Layer 1 - Brand world

Question.
What emotional world should the app live in?

Possible answers.
- Premium cinematic night mode.
- Premium cinematic night mode with playful cyberpunk accents.
- Aggressive neon futurism.
- Curated editorial movie-club feel.

Why it matters.
This determines whether the app feels expensive, moody, funny, intimate, dramatic, or generic.

Current recommendation.
Premium cinematic night mode with playful cyberpunk accents.

### Layer 2 - Signature moment

Question.
What is the one moment people remember emotionally?

Possible answers.
- TV-static loading sting.
- Result reveal animation.
- Handoff transition.
- Poster card reveal in the reaction flow.

Why it matters.
Top apps usually have one or two moments that are not generic app kit behavior.

Current recommendation.
TV-static loading sting plus a polished result reveal.

### Layer 3 - Visual density

Question.
How information-rich should each screen feel?

Possible answers.
- Minimal and sparse.
- Medium density with strong hierarchy.
- Rich and immersive with controlled disclosure.

Why it matters.
This decides whether the app feels luxurious, efficient, or overwhelming.

Current recommendation.
Medium density with strong hierarchy.

### Layer 4 - Typography strategy

Question.
Should the typography feel neutral, editorial, or graphic?

Possible answers.
- Neutral premium sans.
- Sans main type with one stylized accent family for signature moments.
- Strong graphic display type throughout.

Why it matters.
Typography is one of the fastest ways to make an app feel current or dated.

Current recommendation.
Neutral premium sans for product UI, with one stylized accent family reserved for intro or reveal moments.

### Layer 5 - Material system

Question.
What do surfaces feel like?

Possible answers.
- Flat and matte.
- Soft glass and glow.
- Hard neon panels.
- Poster-led layered depth.

Why it matters.
This defines whether the app feels physical, digital, cold, warm, premium, or gimmicky.

Current recommendation.
Poster-led layered depth with soft glass and controlled glow.

### Layer 6 - Control language

Question.
How should controls feel in the hand?

Possible answers.
- Soft rounded premium controls.
- Pill-heavy playful controls.
- Crisp angular controls.

Why it matters.
Controls are the repeated tactile language of the product.

Current recommendation.
Soft rounded premium controls.

### Layer 7 - Progress language

Question.
How should the app show where the couple is in the flow?

Possible answers.
- Explicit chips for each step.
- Single progress bar with current-step label.
- Minimal line with subtle markers.

Why it matters.
This strongly affects whether the flow feels clean or process-heavy.

Current recommendation.
Single progress bar with current-step label.

### Layer 8 - Poster role

Question.
How important is movie artwork in the composition?

Possible answers.
- Functional thumbnail.
- Primary hero surface on reaction cards and results.
- Background-only atmospheric support.

Why it matters.
If poster art is not treated seriously, the app can feel abstract and generic instead of cinematic.

Current recommendation.
Primary hero surface on reaction cards and results.

### Layer 9 - Motion philosophy

Question.
Where should motion carry the premium feeling?

Possible answers.
- Mostly static with subtle transitions.
- Cinematic transitions on key moments only.
- Motion-heavy throughout.

Why it matters.
Too little motion feels flat.
Too much motion feels juvenile or exhausting.

Current recommendation.
Cinematic transitions on key moments only.

### Layer 10 - Humor level

Question.
How much personality and humor should the product show?

Possible answers.
- Almost none.
- Dry, occasional wit.
- Playful branded humor in key moments.
- Constant joke-first voice.

Why it matters.
Humor can make the product lovable, but too much can make it feel unserious.

Current recommendation.
Playful branded humor in key moments only.

## The next real decisions we still need

These are the decisions we have not fully locked yet.

1. How bold should the launch sting really be?
2. Should the main UI use one accent font anywhere outside the intro and reveal moments?
3. How much glow is acceptable before the app crosses into gimmick territory?
4. How poster-led should the reaction and result screens become?
5. Should the result reveal feel quiet and premium or dramatic and celebratory?
6. How much playful copy should survive outside onboarding and loading?
7. How much visual asymmetry are we willing to allow in layouts before scanability suffers?

## Execution implication

If we want a world-class leap in feel, the next artifact should not be another token board.
It should be a set of genuinely high-fidelity concept screens that deliberately test these open decisions.

## Plain-English summary

We have already identified many useful UI decisions.
The missing layer is art direction.
That means deciding not just what a button looks like, but what emotional world the product belongs to, where the signature moments live, how dramatic it should be, and how much personality it should show.
