# High-Fidelity Concept Extraction Brief

## Purpose

This brief translates the current high-fidelity concept direction into a critique and implementation-preparation artifact.
It exists so the team can preserve what feels magical in the concept art while still making grounded product decisions for the real app.

## Source concept

The current north-star concept is the first high-fidelity concept board generated for Movie Night Mediator on June 28, 2026.
This brief does not treat the concept as literal implementation.
It treats it as a direction to extract from carefully.

## How to critique this concept well

When reviewing a high-fidelity concept, do not start with “do I like it?”
Start with “what exactly is working?”

Pay attention to these six things.

### 1. Emotional first impression

Question.
What does the screen make you feel in the first two seconds?

What to watch for.
- Does it feel expensive?
- Does it feel cinematic?
- Does it feel playful in the right way?
- Does it feel like an actual consumer app rather than a prototype?

Bad critique example.
`I don't know, maybe I just don't like it.`

Good critique example.
`This feels premium and memorable, but a bit too nightclub-heavy for a shared couch tool.`

### 2. Hierarchy

Question.
Do your eyes go to the right place first, second, and third?

What to watch for.
- What grabs you first?
- Is the primary action obvious?
- Does supporting info stay supporting?
- Is anything visually shouting too loudly?

Bad critique example.
`There is too much on the screen.`

Good critique example.
`The poster and title are perfect, but the supporting metadata is still competing too much with the action buttons.`

### 3. Ownable product identity

Question.
Does this feel like Movie Night Mediator, or just a nice-looking app?

What to watch for.
- Is there a memorable signature moment?
- Does the TV-static idea feel distinctive?
- Does the overall world feel like it belongs specifically to this product?

Bad critique example.
`Looks cool.`

Good critique example.
`The loading sting feels ownable, but the results screen still feels like it could belong to any recommendation app.`

### 4. Usefulness under the beauty

Question.
If you were actually picking a movie with your wife, would this help or slow you down?

What to watch for.
- Can you scan the screen quickly?
- Is the information actually helping the decision?
- Is any part beautiful but impractical?

Bad critique example.
`I like the art style.`

Good critique example.
`The reaction screen looks much better, but I still need one clearer line telling me why this movie fits tonight.`

### 5. Restraint

Question.
Is the design intense in the right places only?

What to watch for.
- Too much glow.
- Too much color activity.
- Too much stylized type.
- Too many visually “special” things happening at once.

Bad critique example.
`Make it even crazier.`

Good critique example.
`The intro earns the dramatic glow, but the same intensity should not carry into every utility state.`

### 6. Repeatability

Question.
Can this visual idea survive across the whole product?

What to watch for.
- Can the design system scale to onboarding, reaction flow, results, and history?
- Is the concept relying on one hero image too heavily?
- Will repeated use still feel good on the fifth session?

Bad critique example.
`This one screen looks amazing.`

Good critique example.
`The launch and startup look incredible, but we need to be sure the same material language still works on denser screens.`

## What to focus on screen by screen

### Screen 1 - Launch/loading sting

What matters most.
- Does it feel ownable?
- Is the humor level right?
- Does it build anticipation without feeling cheesy?
- Is it short enough to feel premium rather than obstructive?

Open questions.
- Should `to save your marriage` stay this direct?
- Should the intro feel more elegant and less wink-heavy?
- Should the static effect be visually sharper or softer?

### Screen 2 - Startup screen

What matters most.
- Does this feel like a modern flagship app?
- Is the main next action crystal clear?
- Does the world feel premium and cinematic without confusing the user?
- Is the information density right?

Open questions.
- Should the startup be even cleaner?
- Should the hero visual be more understated?
- Should the date-led header remain the main framing device?

Product-control note to revisit later.
- `Language` should be changeable from startup or setup with at least:
  - `English`
  - `Foreign audio + English subtitles`
  - `No rules`
- `People` should be changeable with at least:
  - `Husband`
  - `Wife`
  - `Husband + Wife`

### Screen 3 - Reaction card

What matters most.
- Does the poster-led layout feel exciting and helpful?
- Are the action controls easy to understand quickly?
- Does the explanation feel useful rather than decorative?
- Does the screen feel like a real product interaction and not just pretty concept art?

Open questions.
- How much detail should be visible by default?
- How much should live behind `More details`?
- How dominant should the poster remain on smaller screens or with weaker art?

Locked critique notes so far.
- Founder response: this is finally the right level.
- Include a Rotten Tomatoes-style score or similar fast confidence cue on the reaction card.
- `Seen it` should not appear as a peer choice to `Interested / Maybe / No`.
- The memory action must be visually separated from the main tonight-rating controls.
- Preferred label for the separated memory action: `Also seen this`.

### Screen 4 - Results reveal

What matters most.
- Does the payoff feel satisfying?
- Is the best pick clearly the hero?
- Are fallback options visible without stealing the moment?
- Does it make you want to trust the recommendation?

Open questions.
- Should the results feel more celebratory or more composed?
- How much score language should remain visible?
- Should backup picks be immediate or tucked behind a secondary interaction?

Locked critique notes so far.
- Founder response: this feels satisfying and premium and is the right payoff direction.
- The core match visual should respond to match strength rather than remain static.
- A heart-style indicator is a promising direction if it clearly changes with recommendation confidence.
- Example behavior to explore:
  - very high match: faster beat, warmer or redder emphasis
  - strong match: calmer but positive, possibly green or another approved success tone
  - lower but acceptable match: more restrained state
- The result card needs a dedicated space for a short, LLM-generated shared-why explanation.
- The long-term goal is a short plain-language line such as:
  - `You both tend to say yes to tense sci-fi with emotional payoff.`
  - `This hits a shared sweet spot: big stars, strong momentum, and low setup friction.`

## Extraction targets

These are the things most worth preserving from the concept.

### Preserve

- The sense of a coherent nighttime product world.
- The stronger compositional hierarchy.
- The use of poster art as a primary cinematic surface.
- The feeling that the app has a signature intro moment.
- The cleaner distinction between hero content and support content.
- The more premium spacing and depth.

### Be careful with

- Overusing glow.
- Overusing playful copy.
- Letting decorative effects crowd actual decision-making.
- Making every screen equally dramatic.

### Translate, do not copy literally

- The exact concept text.
- The exact lighting effect intensity.
- Any decorative element that does not survive real content.
- Any composition that only works because the concept art uses idealized imagery.

## The next decisions this concept should help us make

1. Brand humor intensity.
2. Launch sting boldness.
3. Poster dominance on reaction and results screens.
4. Default information density on the reaction card.
5. Result reveal tone: celebratory or composed.
6. Glow intensity in real product UI.

## Suggested review method

For each screen, answer these four questions.

1. What feels definitely right?
2. What feels beautiful but risky?
3. What still feels too timid?
4. What would frustrate me in real use?

## Plain-English summary

The goal of this brief is to help critique the concept art intelligently.
The right critique is not “I like it” or “I don't.”
The right critique is identifying what makes it feel premium, what makes it feel ownable, what might harm usability, and what absolutely needs to survive into the real app.
